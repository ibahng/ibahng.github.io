\section{Alcohol Foundations}

  \begin{definition}{Alcohols}
  
    \textbf{Alcohols} (\ce{ROH}) are a fundamental class of compounds characterized by the presence of one or more hydroxyl (\ce{-OH}) groups attached to a carbon atom.

    Because of this hydroxyl group(s) they possess relatively \textbf{high melting and boiling points} and \textbf{high water solubility} because of their ability to hydrogen bond. However, their water solubility decreases with the size of R because \textbf{R is hydrophobic} and \textbf{-OH is hydrophilic}.

  \end{definition}

  The acidic nature of alcohols is represented by the shell equation \ce{ROH + H2O <=> RO- + H3O+}. Using this equation, here are the $pK_a$s of alcohols across various R groups. \textit{As a reminder, lower $pK_a$ values means higher acidity.}

      \hline

      2 & CH$_3$\textcolor{red}{O}H & 15.5 \\
      3 & CH$_3$CH$_2$\textcolor{red}{O}H & 15.9 \\

  \begin{core}{Solvation and Alcohol Acidity}

    Notice that as the alcohol shifts from methyl $\rightarrow$ primary $\rightarrow$ secondary $\rightarrow$ tertiary R groups (2 $\rightarrow$ 3 $\rightarrow$ 4 $\rightarrow$ 5), the acidity of the alcohol increases.
   
    This pattern is primarily driven by \textbf{solvation} where the negative charge on an alkoxide ion (\ce{RO-}) is stabilized through solvent molecules surrounding the oxygen to spread out the charge. In more sterically hindered instances like \ce{(CH3)3COH}, this solvation occurs to a lesser extent, making the alkoxide form less stable in comparison to methoxide (\ce{CH3OH}).

  \end{core}  

  \begin{core}{Inductive Effect and Alcohol Acidity} 

    The larger presence of electronegative halogen species in ROH increases the acidity of the alcohol due to the \textbf{inductive effect}, which refers to the transmission of a partial electrical charge throughout a molecule's atoms. A resulting alkoxide \ce{RO-} is more stablized when the R group is able to "share the burden" of the concentrated negative charge on the oxygen.

    As a result, compound 7 (\ce{CF3CH2OH}) has a smaller $pK_a$ value compared to compound 6 (\ce{ClCH2CH2OH}). This acidic pattern also occurs with increasing distance between the \ce{-O-} and halogen species, an observation we see in the $pK_a$ differences between compound 7 and 8.

  \end{core}\section{Alcohol Synthesis}

    Direct hydrogenation is possible, but cumbersome because it needs \ce{H2} pressure and catalysts (e.g. Rd, Pt). Instead, the most common laboratory method is via \textbf{hydride} (\ce{H-}) donors, which are prepared either through boron or aluminum via the following reactions.

    \bce{\ce{Na^{+}H- + BH3 -> Na^{+}BH4-}\hspace{5mm} \textit{sodium borohydride}}

    \bce{\ce{Li^{+}H- + AlH3 -> Li^{+}AlH4-}\hspace{5mm} \textit{lithium aluminum hydride}}

    \ce{BH4-} and \ce{AlH40} are still basic by nature and will undergo protonation by alcohols or water (protic) to give \ce{H2}, an undesired reaction pathway. However their reactivities differ. \ce{AlH4-} is a much more aggressive \ce{H-} donor compared to \ce{BH4-} because Al is less electronegative than B, thus the nearby negative charge from \ce{H-} is less stable compared to B. Consequently, \ce{AlH4-} is so reactive that it will react even with air moisture to form \ce{H2}.

    As a result, \ce{Na^{+}BH4-} \textbf{allows us to use alcohol solvents} if needed without worrying about protonation. On the other hand, \ce{Li^{+}AlH4-} requires aprotic or dry solvents to prevent protonation, such as ethers.

    \bce{\ce{Na^{+}BH4- ->[H2O] 4H2 + Na^{+}B(OH)4-}\hspace{5mm} \textit{slow}}

    \bce{\ce{Li^{+}AlH4- ->[H2O] 4H2 + Li^{+}Al(OH)4-}\hspace{5mm} \textit{violent}}

    The donated \ce{H-} will thus attack the central carbon of the ketone or aldehyde, resulting in an alkoxide, which is then protonated by an alcohol solvent (for \ce{Na^{+}BH4-}) or aqueous work up (for \ce{Li^{+}AlH4-}). \textit{Mind that the aqeuous work up is in a separate step after purification. test change}

  \begin{core}{Organometallics \rightarrow Alcohols}
   
    Alcohol can be synthesized from \textbf{organometallics}, namely \textbf{grignard reagents} (\ce{RMgX}) and organolithium reagents (\ce{RLi}). Just as \ce{BH4-} and \ce{LiH4-} are were used to donate \ce{H-} to push the π bond \ce{e^-}s onto the oxygen, grignard reagents do the same but donates an entire \ce{R-} rather than \ce{H-}.

    We can synthesize organolithium reagents and grignard reagents via the following chemical pathways.

    \bce{\ce{Li + RX ->[hexane][-X] R-Li + LiX} \textit{organolithium reagents}}

    \bce{\ce{Mg + RX ->[ether] R-Mg-X} \textit{grignard reagents}}

    Due to electronegativity differences organolithium reagents must be done in a nonreactive, nonpolar solvent like hexane. Grignard reagents, on the other hand, must be done in ethers to prevent \ce{RMgX} from clumping up via solvation.

  \begin{core}{Hydride Shifts}
     
    \textbf{Hydride shifts} refer to the movement of a intramolecular hydrogen towards a neighboring empty p orbital.

    \myfig{0.7}{alcohols_figures/ar_hshift}{Hydride Shift Mechanism in SN1 Reaction}{fig:ar_hshift} % KEEP

    This is particularly an issue if the shift is favorable, such as the move from a \ce{R_{sec}+} to a \ce{R_{tert}+}. Degenerate shifts (\ce{R_{sec}+} $\rightarrow$ \ce{R_{sec}+} or \ce{R_{tert}+} $\rightarrow$ \ce{R_{tert}+}) are less common, but can still occur, resulting in the halide attacking at a unintended location.

      \scheme{

        \chemname{
          \chemfig{

            -[:0]\textcolor{Green}{\textbf{C}}

  So, how do we actually turn an ROH into RNu without all of these potential issues that are bound to happen? The solution is to use inorganic derivatives as reactive or isolable intermediates.
