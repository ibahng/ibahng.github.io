\section{Meiosis and Sexual Life Cycles}

  \subsection{Sexual Life Cycles}

    The transmission of traits from one generation to the next is called inheritance, or \textbf{heredity}. However, offspring are not identical copies of their parents or of one another. Along with inherited similarity, there is also \textbf{variation}. The study of both heredity and inherited variation is called \textbf{genetics}. Parents pass on coded biological information to their offspring in the form of hereditary units called \textbf{genes}, which are distinct segments of DNA that encode specific functional products.

    Let us first revisit somatic cells and their chromosomes. In humans, somatic cells contain two sets of chromosomes—--one inherited from the father and one from the mother--—for a total of 46 chromosomes. Cells with this two-set (2n) chromosome complement are called \textbf{diploid}. \textit{Note that the diploid chromosome number varies among species.} Below is a chromosome diagram of a human somatic cell called a \textbf{karyotype}.

    \myfig{0.4}{evolution_figures/karyotype}{caption}{fig:fig1}

    As shown, the two chromosomes in each pair have the same length, centromere position, and overall gene arrangement. These are called \textbf{homologous chromosomes}. Both chromosomes of a pair carry genes that control the same inherited characteristics, although the specific versions of those genes (alleles) may differ. This pattern applies to 22 of the 23 chromosome pairs. The remaining pair consists of the \textbf{sex chromosomes}, which are typically XX in females and XY in males. The other 22 chromosome pairs are called the \textbf{autosomes}.

    In the context of \textbf{sexual reproduction}, \textbf{gametes} are the cells that transmit genes from one generation to the next. Gametes are \textbf{haploid} (n), meaning they contain one set of chromosomes, and are produced by a process called \textbf{meiosis}. An offspring is formed through the fusion of a male gamete (sperm) and a female gamete (egg), restoring the diploid (2n) chromosome number. This fusion process is called \textbf{fertilization}.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/sexual_life_cycle}{caption}{fig:fig2}

    This differs from \textbf{asexual reproduction}, in which offspring arise from a single individual through mitotic cell division, producing genetically identical daughter cells, or \textbf{clones}.

    This pattern of reproduction is called a \textbf{sexual life cycle}. While we are most familiar with the human life cycle, many organisms exhibit different forms of sexual life cycles.

    Plants and some algae exhibit a life cycle called \textbf{alternation of generations}, which includes multicellular diploid and haploid stages. The diploid \textbf{sporophyte} undergoes meiosis to produce haploid \textbf{spores}. Unlike gametes, spores do not fuse but divide by mitosis to form a multicellular haploid \textbf{gametophyte}. The gametophyte produces gametes by mitosis, and fertilization between two gametes forms a diploid zygote that develops into the next sporophyte. Thus, the sporophyte gives rise to the gametophyte, and the gametophyte gives rise to the sporophyte, \textit{alternating between generations}.

    \myfig{0.4}{evolution_figures/alt_cycle1}{caption}{fig:fig3}

    Most fungi and some protists, including certain algae, exhibit a life cycle in which the \textbf{only diploid stage is the single-celled zygote}. After gametes fuse to form a diploid zygote, \textbf{meiosis occurs immediately}, producing haploid cells rather than a multicellular diploid organism. These haploid cells divide by mitosis to form either unicellular descendants or a multicellular haploid adult. The haploid organism then produces gametes by mitosis, completing the cycle.

    \myfig{0.4}{evolution_figures/alt_cycle2}{caption}{fig:fig4}
  
  \subsection{Meiosis}

    \textbf{Meiosis} is the process by which specialized cells called \textbf{germ cells} in the gonads (ovaries in females and testes in males) produce gametes. Like mitosis, meiosis is preceded by interphase, which includes the S phase during which chromosomes are replicated. However, meiosis consists of two consecutive cell divisions--—meiosis I and meiosis II—--resulting in four haploid daughter cells rather than two diploid daughter cells.

    Each meiotic division includes prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase (followed by cytokinesis). These stages are designated with Roman numerals (I or II) to indicate whether they occur during meiosis I or meiosis II.

    \myfig{1}{evolution_figures/meiosis1}{caption}{fig:fig5}

    (1) \textbf{PROPHASE I:} The centrosomes move apart, the spindle forms, the nuclear envelope breaks down, and kinetochores develop. \textbf{Crossing over}—--the exchange of DNA between nonsister chromatids of homologous chromosomes--—occurs, resulting in the formation of visible structures called \textbf{chiasmata}.

    (2) \textbf{METAPHASE I:} Pairs of \textbf{homologous chromosomes} align at the metaphase plate. Each homologous pair aligns independently of the other pairs, contributing to \textbf{independent assortment}.

    (3) \textbf{ANAPHASE I:} Proteins responsible for sister chromatid cohesion along the chromosome arms are cleaved, allowing homologous chromosomes to separate. However, sister chromatids remain attached at their centromeres.

    (4) \textbf{TELOPHASE I/CYTOKINESIS:} A cleavage furrow forms in animal cells, dividing the cell into two daughter cells, each containing a haploid set of duplicated chromosomes.

    \myfig{1}{evolution_figures/meiosis2}{caption}{fig:fig6}
    
    (5) \textbf{PROPHASE II:} The spindle apparatus forms, the nuclear envelope breaks down if it has re-formed, and kinetochores attach to spindle microtubules.

    (6) \textbf{METAPHASE II:} Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate. Due to crossing over in meiosis I, the two sister chromatids of each chromosome are not genetically identical. Kinetochores of sister chromatids attach to microtubules extending from opposite poles.

    (7) \textbf{ANAPHASE II:} Proteins holding the sister chromatids together at the centromere are cleaved, allowing the chromatids to separate and move toward opposite poles. Each separated chromatid is now considered an individual chromosome.

    (8) \textbf{TELOPHASE II/CYTOKINESIS:} Nuclear envelopes re-form around each set of chromosomes, which begin to decondense. Cytokinesis then occurs.

    This entire process produces four daughter cells, each containing a haploid set of unduplicated chromosomes that are genetically distinct from one another and from the original parent cell.

    One key distinction between meiosis and mitosis is the occurrence of \textbf{crossing over} during prophase I. Crossing over is the reciprocal exchange of corresponding DNA segments between nonsister chromatids of homologous chromosomes.

    \myfig{0.6}{evolution_figures/crossing_over}{caption}{fig:fig7}

    The visible sites where crossing over has occurred are called \textbf{chiasmata}. At least one crossover event per homologous chromosome pair is typically required to ensure proper alignment and separation during meiosis I, as chiasmata help hold homologous chromosomes together until they are pulled apart at anaphase I.

  \subsection{Genetic Variation}

    Three mechanisms contribute to the genetic variation arising from sexual reproduction.

    (1) The \textbf{independent assortment of chromosomes} refers to the random orientation of pairs of \textbf{homologous chromosomes} at the metaphase plate during \textbf{metaphase I} of meiosis.

    \myfig{0.6}{evolution_figures/metaphase1}{caption}{fig:fig8}

    Each homologous pair can orient with either the maternal homolog or the paternal homolog facing a given pole. Thus, there is a 50\% probability that a daughter cell of meiosis I will receive the maternal chromosome of a particular pair and a 50\% probability that it will receive the paternal chromosome.

    Because each of the 23 homologous chromosome pairs in humans assort independently of the others, this mechanism produces $2^{23}$ (over 8 million) possible combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes in a single gamete---even before the next two factors are considered.

    (2) The \textbf{random nature of fertilization} further increases genetic variation. Since any one of the $2^{23}$ different chromosomal combinations in a male gamete can fuse with any one of the $2^{23}$ different chromosomal combinations in a female gamete, the resulting zygote can have $2^{46}$ (over 70 trillion) possible combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes.

    Importantly, this calculation considers only independent assortment and random fertilization.

    (3) \textbf{Crossing over} during prophase I of meiosis I produces \textbf{recombinant chromosomes} that are genetically distinct from the original maternal and paternal homologs. During this process, nonsister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange corresponding DNA segments at sites called chiasmata. Because crossing over can occur at many different positions along each homologous pair—--and may occur multiple times per pair—--the number of possible genetic combinations increases dramatically.

\section{Mendelian Genetics}

  \subsection{Inheritance and Mendel's Peas}

    Around 1857, Gregor Mendel, an Austrian monk, began conducting experiments on garden peas in the monastery garden in Brno. He focused on traits that existed in two distinct forms and began with \textbf{true-breeding} plants, which produce identical offspring through repeated self-pollination. He then performed \textbf{hybridization} by crossing two contrasting true-breeding varieties, designating them as the \textbf{P generation} (parental generation). Their offspring constituted the \textbf{F1 generation}. Mendel allowed the F1 plants to self-pollinate, producing the \textbf{F2 generation}. By analyzing patterns across the P, F1, and especially the F2 generations, Mendel deduced the fundamental principles of inheritance which we will discuss shortly.

    \myfig{0.8}{evolution_figures/mendel_flowers}{caption}{fig:fig9}

    Shown above are the results of one of Mendel's experiments examining flower color.

    At the time, the prevailing \textit{blending hypothesis} proposed that parental traits mix like paint, predicting uniform intermediate offspring that would persist across generations. However, Mendel's experiments contradicted this idea. Crossing true-breeding purple- and white-flowered plants produced all-purple F1 offspring rather than an intermediate color. When the F1 plants self-pollinated, white flowers reappeared in the F2 generation in an approximate 3:1 ratio (purple:white). These results demonstrated that traits are inherited as discrete units (now known as genes), with \textbf{dominant} units masking the expression of \textbf{recessive} units rather than blending with them.    

    Let us now examine four related concepts Mendel used to explain his model of inheritance.

    (1) Alternative versions of genes account for variations in inherited characters.

    (2) For each character, an organism inherits two versions (that is, two \textbf{alleles}) of a gene, one from each parent.

    (3) If the two alleles at a given locus differ, one—--the \textbf{dominant} allele (denoted by an uppercase letter, e.g., purple or \textit{P})—--determines the organism's phenotype. The other—--the \textbf{recessive} allele (denoted by a lowercase letter, e.g., white or \textit{p})—--does not affect the phenotype in the presence of the dominant allele.

    (4) The two alleles for a heritable character segregate during gamete formation and end up in different gametes.

    This fourth point is known as the \textbf{law of segregation}. It can be illustrated using a \textbf{Punnett square}, which shows all possible allele combinations in the offspring of two parents for a given character, assuming their genotypes are known.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/punnett}{caption}{fig:fig10}

    Before moving on, let us review some important genetic vocabulary.

    An organism that has two identical alleles for a gene is called a \textbf{homozygote} and is said to be \textbf{homozygous} for that gene (\textit{PP} or \textit{pp}). In contrast, an organism that has two different alleles for a gene is called a \textbf{heterozygote} and is said to be \textbf{heterozygous} (\textit{Pp}).

    Because dominant and recessive alleles can produce the same observable trait in different genetic combinations, an organism's appearance does not always reveal its genetic composition. We therefore distinguish between an organism's observable traits, its \textbf{phenotype}, and its genetic makeup, its \textbf{genotype}.

    Using these definitions: \textit{PP} represents a homozygous dominant genotype with a dominant phenotype; \textit{Pp} represents a heterozygous genotype with a dominant phenotype; and \textit{pp} represents a homozygous recessive genotype with a recessive phenotype. Notice that the same phenotype does not necessarily correspond to the same genotype.

    If an organism displays a dominant phenotype, we cannot determine whether its genotype is homozygous dominant (\textit{PP}) or heterozygous (\textit{Pp}) based on appearance alone. To determine its genotype, we can perform a \textbf{testcross} by mating it with an organism that is homozygous recessive (\textit{pp}), which always donates the recessive phenotype.

    If any offspring display the recessive phenotype, the unknown parent must be heterozygous (\textit{Pp}), since it contributed the recessive allele. If all offspring display the dominant phenotype, the unknown parent is most likely homozygous dominant (\textit{PP}).

    \myfig{0.6}{evolution_figures/testcross}{caption}{fig:fig11}

    The second law Mendel derived from his experiments is the \textbf{law of independent assortment}, which states that alleles of different genes assort independently of one another during gamete formation.

    So far, we have discussed crosses involving only a single character. The F1 offspring from two contrasting true-breeding parents are called \textbf{monohybrids}, meaning they are heterozygous for the one specific character being studied.

    Mendel also examined two characters simultaneously, such as seed color (yellow/\textit{Y} or green/\textit{y}) and seed shape (round/\textit{R} or wrinkled/\textit{r}). When he crossed true-breeding parents for both traits (\textit{YYRR} x \textit{yyrr}), the F1 offspring were \textbf{dihybrids}, individuals heterozygous for both characters (\textit{YyRr}).

    If the two genes were inherited together (i.e., did not assort independently), the F2 offspring from crossing F1 heterozygotes would show a 3:1 phenotypic ratio for each combined trait pattern. However, when genes assort independently, the F2 phenotypic ratio follows the characteristic 9:3:3:1 pattern observed in a \textbf{dihybrid cross}.

    The actual results of the experiment supported the independent over the dependent assortment hypothesis.
    
    \myfig{0.8}{evolution_figures/dihybrid}{caption}{fig:fig12}
  
  \subsection{Complex Inheritance Patterns}

   Although Mendel laid a foundational explanation for inheritance patterns observed for simple traits, not all heritable characteristics were determined so simply, and the relationship between genotype and phenotype is rarely so straightforward. In this section, we will cover the more complex inheritance patterns that are observed in nature.

   First we will consider extensions of Mendelian genetics for a single gene.
   
   (1) \textbf{Complete dominance:} In Mendel's classic pea crosses, the F1 offspring always looks like one of the two parental varieties because one allele in a pair showed complete complete dominance over the other
   
   (2) \textbf{Incomplete dominance:} For some genes, neither allele is completely dominant over the other, and thus the F1 hybrids would have a phenotype that is a blend of the two parental phenotypes. For example, true-breeding red and white peas would have an F1 hybrid that is phenotypically pink.

     \myfig{0.4}{evolution_figures/incomplete_dominance}{caption}{fig:fig13}
   
   (3) \textbf{Codominance:} In this variation, the two alleles each affect the phenotype in separate, distinguishable ways. For example, the human MN blood group is determined by codominant alleles for two specific molecules locaed on the surface of red blood cells, the M and N moleucules. While a homozygous individual would only express the M or N molecule, a heterozygous individual would express both molecules. Below is an example of codominance using the same flower color trait.

     \myfig{0.3}{evolution_figures/codominance}{caption}{fig:fig14}

   It's important to note here that \textbf{whether a certain gene displays incomplete dominance or codominance is also dependent on the level at which the phenotype is analyzed}. Take the heterozygous pink flower for example shown in incomplete dominance. While from afar it may look uniformly pink, actual pigment molecules that are present may be just a uniform mix of both red and white which would indicate codominance. From a simple visual level, this gene shows incomplete dominance, but from a molecular level it shows codominance.
   
   (4) \textbf{Multiple Alleles:} Most genes have more than two alleles. The ABO blood group in humans, for instance, are determined by the two alleles a person has of the blood group gene; the three possible alleles are $I^A$, $I^B$, and $i$.

     \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/multiple_alleles}{caption}{fig:fig15}
   
   (5) \textbf{Pleiotropy:} Some genes also have multiple phenotypic effects. For instance. the gene that determines the flower color in the pea also affects the color of the coating on the outer surface of the seed, which can be gray or white.

   Now let consider extensions of Mendelian genetics for two or more genes.
   
   (1) \textbf{Epistasis:} The phenotypic expression of a gene at one \textbf{locus} (the location of the gene on the chromosome) alters that of a gene at a second locus. In Labrador retrievers, black coat color (\textit{B}) is dominant to brown (\textit{b}). A second gene determines whether or not pigment will be deposited in the hair. The dominant allele, symbolized by E, result sin the deposition of either black or brown pigment, depending on the genetype f the first locus. But if the Lab is \textit{ee}, then the coat is yellow, regardless of the genotype at the black/brown locus.

     \myfig{0.44}{evolution_figures/epistasis}{caption}{fig:fig16}
   
   (2) \textbf{Polygenic Inheritance:} Many characteristics, like skin color and height, are not one of two discrete characters, but instead vary in the population in gradations along a continuum. These are called \textbf{quantitative characters} that depend on the expression of several genes. Below is a simplifed example of skin color uding only three genes.

     \myfig{0.44}{evolution_figures/polygenic_inheritance}{caption}{fig:fig17}

   Although genes and genotypes are the foundation of an individual's phenotype, certain characteristics like skin color that we've seen just now are also influenced by environmental factors, such as exposure to sun. Geneticits refer to such characters as \textbf{multifactorial}, meaning that many factors, both genetic and environmental, collectively influence phenotype.
  
  \subsection{Use Cases for Mendelian Patterns of Inheritance}

    Rather than performing controlled breeding experiments, geneticists analyze human inheritance patterns by constructing a family tree called a \textbf{pedigree}, which traces the transmission of a particular trait across generations.

    \myfig{0.6}{evolution_figures/pedigree}{caption}{fig:fig18}

    The pedigree shown above illustrates inheritance of a recessive trait (e.g., widow's peak). Although this is a simple example, pedigrees are especially important when studying alleles that cause serious or life-threatening diseases.

    In \textbf{recessive disorders}, heterozygous individuals (\textit{Aa}) typically have a normal phenotype because one functional allele produces enough protein for normal function. These individuals are called \textbf{carriers} because they can pass the recessive allele to their offspring. Homozygous recessive individuals (\textit{aa}) express the disorder, having inherited a recessive allele from each parent.

    Most individuals with recessive disorders are born to parents who are carriers but phenotypically normal. In contrast, individuals affected by a lethal recessive disorder often do not reproduce, which helps keep such alleles relatively rare in the population.

    In \textbf{dominant disorders}, heterozygous individuals are phenotypically affected. An example is \textbf{achondroplasia}, a form of dwarfism. Individuals without achondroplasia are homozygous recessive. Because this condition is not typically lethal and does not prevent reproduction, the allele can persist in the population.

    For dominant alleles that cause lethal diseases, the phenotype is usually rare because affected individuals often die before reproducing. However, a dominant lethal allele can still be transmitted if symptoms appear after reproductive age. For example, \textbf{Huntington's disease} is a neurodegenerative disorder caused by a dominant allele whose effects typically appear in mid-adulthood.

    Thus far, we have focused on simple Mendelian disorders caused by mutations at a single genetic locus. However, many common diseases have a \textbf{multifactorial} basis, involving both genetic predisposition and environmental influences. For instance, susceptibility to heart disease or stroke depends not only on genotype but also strongly on lifestyle and other environmental factors.

\section{The Chromosomal Basis of Inheritance}
    
  \subsection{Sex-Linked Genes}

    When Mendel described the laws of inheritance in 1866, chromosomes had not yet been identified as the carriers of genetic information. It was not until 1902 that scientists began to formulate the \textbf{chromosome theory of inheritance}. This theory states that Mendelian genes occupy specific loci (sites) on chromosomes, and that the behavior of chromosomes during meiosis--—specifically segregation and independent assortment—--accounts for the inheritance patterns Mendel observed.

    The first strong evidence linking a specific gene to a specific chromosome came in the early 1900s from the work of Thomas Hunt Morgan. Morgan studied a species of fruit fly, \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, focusing on inheritance of eye color.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/fly_eye_color}{caption}{fig:fig19}

    In wild populations, the most common eye color is red, referred to as the \textbf{wild-type} phenotype and denoted as $w^+$. After two years of breeding flies, Morgan discovered a single male with white eyes instead of red. This trait was designated the \textbf{mutant phenotype} and denoted as $w$.
    
    When Morgan crossed his white-eyed male fly with red-eyed females, all of the F1 offspring had red eyes, indicating that the wild-type allele is dominant over the white-eye allele. When the F1 flies were interbred, the F2 generation displayed an overall 3:1 red-to-white phenotypic ratio. However, a closer look revealed that the white-eye phenotype appeared only in males: approximately half of the males were white-eyed and half were red-eyed, while all females had red eyes. This sex-specific pattern led Morgan to conclude that the eye-color gene is located on the X chromosome.

    Because all F1 offspring were phenotypically wild type, the F1 males (XY) must have carried a wild-type allele on their single X chromosome. The F1 females, however, must have been heterozygous, carrying one wild-type allele and one mutant allele. The appearance of white-eyed males in the F2 generation confirms this: sons inherit their single X chromosome exclusively from their mother. Therefore, any white-eyed male must have received the mutant allele from a heterozygous F1 female, demonstrating that she was a carrier of the recessive white-eye allele.
    
    Humans and other mammals possess two sex chromosomes, X and Y. Females typically inherit two X chromosomes (XX), whereas males inherit one X and one Y (XY). The Y chromosome is considerably smaller than the X chromosome, and only short regions at their ends are homologous. These homologous regions allow the X and Y chromosomes to pair and properly segregate during meiosis in males.

    During gamete formation, the sex chromosomes separate so that every egg carries a single X chromosome, while sperm carry either an X or a Y chromosome. Fertilization by an X-bearing sperm produces an XX zygote (female), whereas fertilization by a Y-bearing sperm produces an XY zygote (male). As a result, sex determination in mammals is typically an approximately 1:1 probability. However, the mammalian X-Y system represents only one of several mechanisms of sex determination found in nature.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/alt_systems}{caption}{fig:fig20}
    
    A gene located on a sex chromosome is called a \textbf{sex-linked gene}. Genes found on the X chromosome are termed \textbf{X-linked}, whereas those on the Y chromosome are \textbf{Y-linked}. The human X chromosome contains approximately 1,100 genes, while the Y chromosome contains about 78 genes that encode roughly 25 distinct proteins, many of which are involved in testis development and sperm production. Because the Y chromosome is transmitted largely unchanged from father to son and carries relatively few genes, comparatively few traits and disorders are inherited in a strictly Y-linked manner.

    X-linked genes follow the inheritance pattern first observed by Morgan in his fruit fly experiments. A father passes his single X chromosome--—and therefore any X-linked alleles—--exclusively to his daughters, not to his sons. Mothers, who possess two X chromosomes, can pass X-linked alleles to both sons and daughters.

    \myfig{1}{evolution_figures/sex_chromo}{caption}{fig:fig21}

    The phenotypic expression of an X-linked trait in females depends on whether the allele is dominant or recessive. Because males possess only one X chromosome, the terms homozygous and heterozygous do not apply to them. Instead, males are described as hemizygous for X-linked genes and will express whatever allele is present on the single X chromosome inherited from their mother.

    Although female mammals inherit two X chromosomes, one is largely inactivated early in embryonic development. This process ensures that males and females have a similar dosage of most X-linked gene products. The inactivated X chromosome condenses into a \textbf{Barr body} at the nuclear periphery, and most of its genes are transcriptionally silent. In the germline cells that give rise to eggs, however, the inactive X is reactivated prior to meiosis so that each egg ultimately contains one active X chromosome.

    As a result of X inactivation, females are genetic \textbf{mosaics}, composed of two populations of cells: those in which the maternal X chromosome is active and those in which the paternal X chromosome is active. Once an X chromosome is inactivated in a particular cell, all of its mitotic descendants maintain the same inactive X. Consequently, a heterozygous female for an X-linked trait will typically have a mixture of cells expressing one allele and cells expressing the alternate allele.

    \myfig{0.52}{evolution_figures/barr_body}{caption}{fig:fig22}

    This phenomenon is also observed in women who are heterozygous for an X-linked mutation that prevents the development of sweat glands, resulting in distinct patches of normal skin interspersed with patches lacking sweat glands.

  \subsection{Linked Genes}

    Genes located near one another on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together during genetic crosses; such genes are described as \textbf{genetically linked} and are referred to as \textbf{linked genes}. Consider the following experiment in which true-breeding flies that are double wild type are crossed with true-breeding flies that are double mutant.

    \myfig{0.85}{evolution_figures/linked_flies}{caption}{fig:fig23}

    If the two genes are located on different chromosomes, independent assortment predicts a 1:1:1:1 phenotypic ratio in the testcross progeny. If the genes are located on the same chromosome and the parental alleles are always inherited together (complete linkage), the expected ratio would be 1:1:0:0, with only parental phenotypes observed. However, the actual data from Morgan's experiment yielded a ratio of 965:944:206:185--—an intermediate result between these two expectations. We now understand that this deviation is due to \textbf{crossing over}, although the precise mechanism was not yet known at the time.

    Before proceeding, it is helpful to clarify key terminology. \textbf{Genetic recombination} refers to the production of offspring with combinations of traits that differ from those present in either parent of the P generation. The trait combinations that match the original parental combinations are called \textbf{parental types}, whereas those that differ are called \textbf{recombinant types}, or simply \textbf{recombinants}.

    Crossing over occurs randomly along homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis. The probability of a crossover occurring between two genes increases with the physical distance separating them. Therefore, the farther apart two genes are on the same chromosome, the greater the likelihood that a crossover will occur between them, resulting in a higher recombination frequency. This frequency approaches 50\%, the value expected for genes that assort independently (as if located on different chromosomes).

    Using recombination frequencies, one of Morgan’s students, Alfred H. Sturtevant, constructed the first genetic maps by assigning relative positions to genes on the same chromosome. These maps, based on recombination data observed in the fruit fly species, are known as \textbf{linkage maps}.

    \myfig{0.7}{evolution_figures/linkage_map}{caption}{fig:fig24}

    Thus, genetic linkage is not a strictly binary property that classifies a pair of genes as simply “linked” or “not linked.” Rather, linkage exists along a continuum and is determined by the physical distance between genes on a chromosome: the closer two genes are, the more tightly linked they are, and the lower their recombination frequency.
  
  \subsection{Chromosomal Disorders}

    So far, we have focused on relatively small-scale genetic changes involving individual genes. However, large-scale chromosomal alterations can also significantly affect an organism's phenotype and may result in miscarriage or developmental disorders.

    First, consider \textit{abnormal chromosome numbers}. These typically arise from an occasional error called \textbf{nondisjunction}, in which homologous chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis I or sister chromatids fail to separate during meiosis II. As a result, some gametes receive an abnormal number of chromosomes.

    \myfig{0.65}{evolution_figures/nondisjunction}{caption}{fig:fig25}

    If an abnormal gamete fuses with a normal one during fertilization, the resulting zygote will have an abnormal chromosome number—--a condition known as \textbf{aneuploidy}. The loss of a single chromosome produces a \textbf{monosomic} zygote ($2n - 1$), whereas the gain of an extra chromosome produces a \textbf{trisomic} zygote ($2n + 1$). Once formed, this chromosomal abnormality is propagated to all somatic cells of the developing embryo through mitotic divisions.

    In contrast, some organisms possess more than two complete chromosome \textbf{sets} (not merely individual extra chromosomes) in their somatic cells. This condition is called \textbf{polyploidy} and includes forms such as \textit{triploidy} ($3n$) and \textit{tetraploidy} ($4n$).    

    In addition to changes in chromosome number, errors during meiosis or exposure to damaging agents such as radiation can \textbf{alter chromosome structure}. These structural abnormalities fall into four major categories:

    (1) \textbf{Deletion} occurs when a chromosomal fragment is lost, leaving the affected chromosome missing certain genes.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/deletion}{caption}{fig:fig26}

    (2) \textbf{Duplication} occurs when a chromosomal fragment becomes attached as an extra segment to a sister chromatid or to a non-sister chromatid, resulting in repeated gene sequences.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/duplication}{caption}{fig:fig27}

    (3) \textbf{Inversion} occurs when a chromosomal fragment breaks off and reattaches to the original chromosome in the reverse orientation, thereby reversing the gene order within that segment.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/inversion}{caption}{fig:fig28}

    (4) \textbf{Translocation} occurs when a chromosomal fragment breaks off and attaches to a nonhomologous chromosome. In some cases, two nonhomologous chromosomes exchange segments, a process known as a reciprocal translocation.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/translocation}{caption}{fig:fig29}

    Now let us examine several well-known conditions that arise from chromosomal abnormalities.

    \textbf{Down syndrome}, also known as \textit{trisomy 21}, is a chromosomal disorder caused by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21.

    \myfig{0.5}{evolution_figures/down_syndrome}{caption}{fig:fig30}

    Aneuploid conditions involving the sex chromosomes tend to disrupt genetic balance less severely than those involving autosomes. This is partly because the Y chromosome contains relatively few genes, and additional X chromosomes are largely inactivated as Barr bodies, reducing their impact on gene expression.

    \textbf{Klinefelter syndrome} is a chromosomal disorder in which individuals possess an extra X chromosome (XXY). Affected individuals are phenotypically male and may exhibit features such as small testes, reduced testosterone levels, taller-than-average height, decreased muscle mass, sparse facial and body hair, and gynecomastia (enlarged breast tissue). Many individuals are infertile.

    Females with \textbf{trisomy X} (XXX) are generally healthy and often have no unusual physical features other than slightly above-average height. However, there is an increased risk of learning disabilities and mild developmental delays.

    \textbf{Turner syndrome} (monosomy X, XO) is the only known viable full monosomy in humans. Individuals with Turner syndrome are phenotypically female but are typically sterile due to underdeveloped ovaries and incomplete sexual maturation.
  
  \subsection{Exceptions to Mendelian Inheritance}

    In the previous section, we examined deviations from typical chromosomal inheritance patterns. However, there are also two naturally occurring exceptions to classical Mendelian inheritance: \textbf{genomic imprinting} and \textbf{inheritance of organelle genes}.

    \textbf{Genomic imprinting} refers to a phenomenon in which the phenotypic effect of an allele depends on whether it was inherited from the mother or the father. For a given imprinted gene, either the maternal or the paternal allele is consistently silenced across individuals of a species, while the other allele is expressed. This parent-specific expression is typically regulated by epigenetic modifications such as DNA methylation established during gamete formation. An example of genomic imprinting is the \textit{insulin-like growth factor 2} (\textit{Igf2}) gene in mice, in which only the paternal allele is expressed.

    \textbf{Inheritance of organelle genes} refers to the transmission of genes located outside the nucleus, specifically the small circular DNA molecules found in mitochondria (and in chloroplasts in plants).

    In most animals—--nd in plants with respect to chloroplast DNA--—a zygote receives nearly all of its cytoplasm and organelles from the egg. The sperm typically contributes little more than a haploid set of nuclear chromosomes. As a result, mitochondrial DNA (and chloroplast DNA in plants) is usually inherited exclusively from the mother, a pattern known as \textit{maternal inheritance}.
