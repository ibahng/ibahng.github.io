\section{Fundamental Concepts}

  Economics study the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services
  Scarcity: There are limited resources to produce goods and services, but wants are unlimited
  Examples of Scarce Resources: Workers, machines
 
  \begin{core}{Four Factors Of Production}

    Four Factors of Production
    - Land (aka Natural Resources): Includes land and other natural goods (ex. land where a factory is built, farmland, water air)
    - Labor: People's effort (ex. farmer harvesting crops, factory worker controlling machines)
    - Capital: Something produced to produce another good (ex. factory building, machines, trucks)
    - Entrepreneurship: A person who combines the other factors of production (ex. business starter, manager)

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Opportunity Cost}

    Opportunity Cost: the value of the best forgone alternative when you choose between mutually exclusive options.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Production Possibilities Curve}

    Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)
    - Efficient
    - Underallocation
    - Overheating (possible in the short run, but not sustainable)
    The shape of the PPC indicates whether there is decreasing, stable, or increasing opportunity cost

    Shifts in PPC
    - Economic growth: PPC shifts outwards
    - Economic decline: PPC shifts inward

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Comparative \& Absolute Advantage}

    Comparative Advantage: When one economy can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than the other
    Absolute Advantage: When one economy can produce more of a good than the other
    - link this to trade between two economies with different opportunity costs across two goods
    - It's impossible for one economy to have a comparative advantage in both goods
    - Each economy should produce the good which it has a comparative advantage in
    - This can also be calculated with the time it takes to produce goods across economies

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Supply \& Demand}

    Demand: the amount of a good that consumers are willing purchase at a certain price
    Law of Demand: A higher price of a good will cause a lower demand for that good
    The determinant of demand are as follows (INSECT)
    - Income
    - Normal goods: D rises as income rises (consistent with income effect)
    - Inferior Goods: D falsl as income rises
    - Numer of Buyers
    - Substitute Goods
    - Substitution Effect: Demand of goods rises as price of a substitute rises
    - Expectation of Future Price: present demand is higher if price is expected to rise
    - Complementary goods: goods that are often used together
    - demand falls as demand of complement falls
    - Taste: the public opinion on the product or service

    Supply: the amount of a good that producers are willing to produce at a certain price
    Law of Supply: a higher price of a good will cause a higher supply for that good
    The determinants of supply are as follows (PETTING)
    - Prices of other goods that firms can produce
    - Supply is higher if price of other goods is lower
    - Expectations of future price
    - Present supple is higher if price is expected to fall
    - Technology
    - Taxes/Subsidy
    - Input Costs
    - Supply falls as input costs rise
    - Number of Sellers
    - Government Regulation

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Market Equilibrium}

    Market Equilibrium: the market automatically adjusts the quantity and price to the intersection of the market supply and demand curves

    Shifts in Equilibrium
    - Change in Demand: A shift of the demand curve due to a change in the determinants of demand
    - Change in Quantity Demanded: A movement along the demand curve due to a change in price
    - Change in Supply: a shift of the supply curve due to the change in the determinants of supply
    - Change in Quantity supplied: a movement along the supply curve

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Circular Flow of Income}

    Circular Flow of Income: 
    - Firms give households wages, rent, dividends and GOODS AND SERVICES
    - Households give firms consumer expenditure and FACTORS FOR PRODUCTION

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Gross Domestic Product}

    \begin{core}{Gross Domestic Product}

      GDP: The market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period
      - GDP does not include transfer payments, such as selling a home constructed in a previous year, stocks

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Economic Cycles}

      The business cycle graph represents the fluctuations in economic expansion and recession in the real GDP in comparison to the potential output of an economy (along the PPC)
      expansion: RGDP rising
      recession: RGDP falling
      peak: local max RGDP
      trough: local min RDGP
      output gap: actual output - potential output at a given time

      A recession is defined as two successive quarters of negative real GDP Growth

    \end{core}

    Calculating GDP

    \begin{core}{Calculating GDP (\textit{Expenditures})}

      Total Expenditures = Total Income = GDP
      Expenditures Approach to Calculate GDP (most common)
      - household consumption (spending on final goods)
      - investment by firms (capital goods and inventory; not including intermediate goods)
      - government purchases (not including transfer payments)
      - net exports (exports - imports)

      GDP = C + I + G + X - M

      I is gross investment
      Net investment = gross investment - depreciation (decrease in value of capital goods)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Calculating GDP (\textit{Income})}

      Income Approach to Calculate GDP
      - GDP = Wages + interest + rents + profits + statistical adjustments
      - Statistical adjustments: depreciation, sales taxes, net foreign factor income

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Calculating GDP (\textit{Value Added})}

      Value added approach

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Limitations of GDP}

      - Doesn't include non market transactions or underground economy
      - ex. a friend (non included) vs taxi (included) drives you to the airport
      - ex. illegally selling fireworks
      - Doesn't capture overall well-being
      - ex. pollution, stress, product quality
      - Doesn't capture depreciation (decrease in value of capital goods)
      - ex. country A and B have similar GDP, but country A spends more to cover depreciation
      - Net domestic product = GDP - Depreciation
      - Doesn't indicate income equality

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Economic Growth}

      Economic Growth
      Economic Growth: A sustained increase in RGDP per capita
      LRAS shifting right
      PPC shifting outward
      Labor Productivity: Output per worker
      The aggregate product function expresses total output as a function of capital stock and human capital (quantity and quality of workers)

      Causes of Economic Growth
      Economic growth is dependent on value of capital stock, human capital, and technological advancement
      Examples
      Companies accumulating capital stock (CS)
      Better education and hence worker skills (HC)
      Increased labor force participation rate (HC)
      Tax credit on businesses for R\&D (TA)
      Government investing in infrastructure such as highways (CS)

    \end{core}

    Inflation and Consumer Price Index

    Inflation is a general rise in price levels (equivalent to money losing value)
    Data sources of inflation include
    CPI = ( Cost of market basket with current prices / cost of market basket with base prices ) * 100
    PCE = personal consumption expenditures of PCE
    Reports that measure prices change in consumer goods and services as well as what sonsumers are spedning their income one
    Inflation expectation surverys (michigan inflation expectation)

    Limitation of CPI: subsitution bias states that if the price of a good goes up by too much, consumers may substitute it with cheaper options

    Inflation Rate = ( Current CPI - Past CPI ) / Past CPI * 100

    %--- Nominal vs Real ---
    NOMINAL VS REAL
    - nominal variables are NOT adjusted for inflation
      - ex: if a bank charges an interst of 5\% annually on a loan, the nominal interest rate is 5\%
    - real variables are adjusted for inflation
      - ex: if a bank charges an interest of 5\% annuall on a loan, but the inflation rate is 3\% over this year, the real interest rate is only 5 - 3 = 2\%

    nominal GDP is determined using current prices
    real GDP is determined using base prices

    GDP deflator = ( nominal GDP / real GDP ) * 100

    Effects of unexpected inflation
    - borrowers benefit from unexpected inflation at the expense of lender
    - ex: a bank may charge a nominal IR of 5 annually on a loan expecting an inflation rate of 3, but if the actual inflation rate was 6, the real interest rate was 5-6=-1 (losing money)

    %--- Nominal vs Real Interest Rate ---
    Nominal vs Real Interest Rate 
    - nominal ir is ir, unadjusted for inflation
    - Expected Real IR = Nominal IR - Expected Inflation Rate
    - Actual Real IR = Nominal IR - Actual Inflation Rate

\section{Macroeconomic Relationships}

  \subsection{Aggregate Demand}

    \begin{core}{Aggregate Demand}

      Aggregate Demand is the total quantity of goods and services demanded in the economy

      If price levels decrease, more will be demanded by households, firms, the government, and other countries
      Reasons
      - Wealth effect: people can purchase more goods and services with their money
      - Interest rate effect: people can spend less and save more, causing interest rates to go down, encouraging borrowing money for investment
      - Exchange rate effect: lower interest rates encourage people to convert out of that currenvy, depreciating that currency, making domestic goods relatively cheaper to foreigners, increasing exports

      Shifts of AD curve are caused by a change in one of the four components of GDP
      - Household consumption
      - if taxes decrease, people have more money they can spend, so AD increases
      - if people are more confident in spending, AD increases
      - Investment by Firms
      - if firms are more confident in investing, AD increases
      - Government Spending
      - if spending increase, AD increases
      - Net exports
      - if other countries demand more, exports and AD increases
      - if a country makes more domestic good and imports less, AD increase

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}{Multiplier Effect}

      Marginal propensity to consume = Δ Consumption spending / Δ disposable income

      marginal propensity to save = Δ Amount saved / Δ Disposable income

      MPC + MPS = 1

      Multiplier Effect
      The MPC in an economy is 0.8
      Albert spends \$10 buying a good from Ben
      Ben spends 0.8*10 = \$8 buying a good from charlie
      Charlie spends 0.8 * \$8  = \$6.40 buying a good from daniel and so on.

      Because of the multiplier effect, total expenditures = total ΔGDP = 10 + 10(0.8) + 10(0.8)(0.8) + ...
      total ΔGDP = 10/(1 - 0.8) = 50

      total ΔGDP = initial spending / 1 - MPC = initial spending / MPS

      To sum up multipliers 
      - spending multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC) 
      - tax multiplier = - ( MPC / ( 1 - MPC ) )
      - rationale for MPC in the numerator for tax: the decrease in inital spending is the tax amount times MPC, not the tax amount itself

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Aggregate Supply}

    \begin{core}{Short Run Aggregate Supply}

      SHORT RUN Aggregate Supply is the total output firms are willing to produce
      If price levels increase, firms are more willing to produce more output in the short run (the period of time where wages and some other input costs are fixed)
      Reason
      - Sticky wages and prices: although prices for a product go up, the production costs are relatively stable, so producing more leads to more profit

      The SRAS cruve shows an incline, representing a trade off between inflation and unemployment. High on the curve represented higher inflation, lower unemployment (economic booms) and lower on the cruve is represented by lower inflation, and higher unemployment (economic recession)

      Shifters of the SRAS curve
      - Productivity (Productivity increases cause rightwards shift)
      - input costs (input costs increasing causes a leftward shift)
      - labor market (more people willing to work causes rightwards shift because wages are suppressed)
      - taxes/subsidies

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Long Run Aggregate Supply}

      Long Run Aggregate Supply
      - In the long run (the period of time where wages and other input costs can adjust to prices changes), output is independent of price levels
      Reason: wages and other input costs adjust to price changes and the profit-maximizing quantity returns to it "natural" level

      The short turn equilibrium is the intersection of AD and SRAS
      - Positive (negative) shock shifts AD or SRAS to the right (left)
      - Demand-pull inflation is cause by positive shock in AD, causeing the intersection to lie higher in the SRAS line
      - Cost-push inflation is cause by a negative shock in SRAS, leading to the same higher intersection location

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Economic Cycles}

    \begin{core}{Long Run Adjustment of Short Run Aggregate Supply}

      Long Run Self Adjustment (No Government Intervention)
      - Without government intervention, SRAS shifts to establish long run equilibrium
      However in the short term
      - Scenario 1: current output < LRAS, wages and input costs fall, causing SRAS to shift to the right
      - Scenario 2: Current output > LRAS, wages and input costs rise, causing SRAS to shift left

      The fluctuations of SRAS are referring to different points along the business cycle graph plotting Real GDP against time. Althorugh the average GDP is represented by a linearly increasing line showing GDP growth, the real GDP number fluctuate above and below this linear line.
      Same thing with the PPC

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Phillips Curve}

      The Phillips Curve shows the tradeoff between unemployment and inflation
      - Movement along SRAS corresponds to movement along the SRPC
      - SRAS shifting right shifts the SRPC to the left (and vice-versa)
      - Natural rate of unemployment changing shifts the LRPC

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Quantity Theory of Money}

      Quantity Theory of Money and Inflation
      MV=PQ
      M is the money supply
      V is the velocity of money (how many times a dollar gets spent on average)
      P is price level (an index)
      Q is quentity of output (real GDP)
      PQ is nominla GDP

      Significance: If the M increase and V and Q stay relatively constant, then P increase in the long run

    \end{core}

\section{Stabilization Policies}

  \subsection{Fiscal Policy}

    \begin{core}{Fisical Policy}

      Fiscal Policy: Controlled by the government

      Fiscal Policy \& Effect on AD
      - Fiscal policy consists of government spending and taxation/transfers in order to shift AD
      - Government can employ contractionary fiscal policy to fix inflation or exapansionary fiscal policy to fix recession
      - Initial shift in AD is multiplied by corresponding multiplier (thus the government spending would be lower than the actual amount they want to shift the AD by)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Automatic Stabilizers}

      Automatic Stabilizers
      - Some fiscal policies automatically counteract inflation or recessions without changing the policies
      - ex: income taxes are an increasing percentage of income based on income brackets, so they automatically rise during periods of inflation (contractionary) and fall during periods of recession
      - this is an advantage over discretionary discal policy: no lag between inflation/recession and change in tax amount

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Crowding Out Effect}

      Deficit and Debt
      Government deficit in a given year equals spending minus tax revenue
      Debt is how much the government owes in total and is equal to accumulated deficits
      Note: The interest on debts is included in each year's spending and therefore budget deficit calculation

      Crowding Out Effect
      A government could use deficit spending to combat a recession. The deficit spending could be funded by borrowing
      Crowding Out Effect: Deficit spending funded by borrowing increases interest rates, decreasing private investment

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Monetary Policy}

    \begin{core}{Monetary Policy}

      Monetary Policy: Controlled by the central bank

      MONETARY POLICY targets the nominal IR and quantity of money held
      - Expansionary monetary policy: decreases nominal IR, increases quantity of money held
      - Contractionary monetary policy: increases nominal IR, decreases quantity of money held

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}{Open Market Operations}

      Open market operations (buying/selling bonds)
      the government buying bonds means increasing the general money supply of the country
      selling means that people are giving the gov money in exchange for these IOUs

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Required Reserve Ratio}

      Required Reserve Ratio
      RRR down = commercial banks loan more = up money supply = expansionary
      RRR up = commercial banks loan less = down money supply = contractionary

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Discount Rate, Interest On Reserves, and Federal Funds Rate}

      - Discount Rate (IR commerical banks can borrow from the central bank for)
      discount rate decreases = commerical banks loan more = increases money supply = expansionary
      discount rate increases = commerical banks loan less = decreases money supply = contractionary

      Interest on Reserves and Federal Funds Rate
      - Starting October 2008, US commercial banks could deposit their reserves to the US central bank (the Federal Reserve) and earn interest (interest on reserves rate)
      - Federal Funds Rate: the interest rate commercial banks charge each other for overnight loans

      When the FFR decreases the quantity of the reserves increases because banks are more inclined to borrow, loosening up the money supply
      - The upper bound to this is the discount rate: commerical banks could borrow from the central bank at that rate, and therefore would not borrow for higher than that rate (gives an IR ceiling for borrowers)
      - The lower bound is the interest on reserves rate: That is the interest commercial banks earn for their reserves, so they would not lend out their reserves at less than that rate (gives an IR floor for lenders) the flat section of this graph is called ample reserves 
      monetary policy tool for ample reserves: cahnge interest on reserves rate
      - down IOR rate = expansionary
      - up IOR rate = contractionary

    \end{core}  

    Fiscal and monetary policy are often in effect simultaneously, and may have opposite effects

      Depreciation: A currency becomes less valuable relative to another

    \begin{core}{Effects on Net Exports}

      If dollar depreciates, US exports increase, imports decrease, and AD increase because European goods are now relatively more expensive to the US and the US goods are now relatively cheaper to Europe
